# Listening — Strategy Guide

> **Status: TBD** — run a full timed Cambridge section before using this strategically.  
> Listening is the most recoverable component for high-proficiency candidates: the ceiling issues are almost never about comprehension, but about attention management and question-type mechanics.

## Test Structure

| Section | Format | Context | Question types |
|---------|--------|---------|---------------|
| 1 | Conversation (2 speakers) | Social / everyday | Form completion, note completion |
| 2 | Monologue | Social / informational | Multiple choice, map/plan labelling |
| 3 | Discussion (up to 4 speakers) | Educational / training | Multiple choice, matching |
| 4 | Monologue | Academic / lecture | Note completion, sentence completion |

- 40 questions total, 1 mark each
- Heard **once only** — no replay
- 10 minutes at the end to transfer answers

## The Core Principle: Anticipate, Don't React

The decisive skill is **reading ahead** — processing the next question while the recording covers the current one. Candidates who listen passively and try to match answers as they hear them consistently miss 3–5 questions due to lag.

**Procedure before the recording starts:**
1. Read all questions in the section
2. Identify the answer type for each (name? number? single word? category?)
3. Underline **keywords** in each question
4. For completion tasks: predict the semantic field (is the answer likely a time, a place, a quantity, a description?)

## Question Type Mechanics

### Form / Note / Table / Flow-chart Completion

**What it tests:** Catching specific information (names, numbers, dates, single words) in continuous speech.

**Key rules:**
- Answers are almost always 1–3 words
- If the instruction says "ONE WORD ONLY", a number written as a digit still counts as one word, but "twenty dollars" would be two
- Spelling counts — the answer must match the recording
- Watch for spelled-out words: the speaker will often spell names letter by letter

**Trap:** The speaker will often give a wrong answer first and then correct it ("It's on the 15th — no, sorry, the 25th"). The answer is always the **corrected** one. At band 7, candidates write the first version.

**Strategy:** For each gap, identify the expected word type before listening. A gap after "Name:" expects a proper noun. A gap after "Cost: £" expects a number. A gap after "Type of..." expects a common noun or category.

---

### Multiple Choice (Single answer)

**What it tests:** Identifying the speaker's actual position, not just hearing a word from an option.

**The distractor mechanism:** All three options will be mentioned in the recording. Only one will be the correct answer to what was **asked**. The other two will be mentioned in a different context — negated, attributed to someone else, described as a past situation, or referred to as rejected alternatives.

**Example pattern:**
> Question: *Why did the speaker choose this location?*
> Option A: It was cheaper (mentioned but rejected: "Although it was cheaper...")
> Option B: It was closer to transport (mentioned as a secondary benefit)
> Option C: It had the facilities they needed (the actual reason given)

**Strategy:** Underline the verb in the question ("chose", "preferred", "decided") and the subject ("the speaker", "the manager", "both students"). The answer must satisfy both.

---

### Multiple Choice (TWO answers from A–E)

**What it tests:** Identifying two correct pieces of information from a set of five.

**The trap:** Candidates often confirm one answer early and stop listening carefully. The second answer may come much later and be stated less emphatically.

**Strategy:** Treat each of the five options as a binary yes/no. Mark a provisional "Y" or "N" as each is addressed. Only finalise after the passage moves on.

---

### Matching (Features / Items to Descriptions)

**What it tests:** Identifying which description applies to which item, often in a list (e.g., matching museums to features, or people to opinions).

**The trap:** The items are presented in order in the recording, but the descriptions (A–G or similar) are not. Candidates sometimes lose track of which description they've already used.

**Strategy:** Write the letter codes in the margin as you go. For "use each letter only once" tasks, cross out each used option. For "you may use any letter more than once" tasks (this is explicitly stated), be alert to repeated attributions.

---

### Plan / Map / Diagram Labelling

**What it tests:** Mapping spoken directions to a visual layout.

**The trap:** Spatial language is dense and sequential — candidates who lose track of the speaker's position in the map cannot recover without starting over.

**Strategy:** Before listening, orient yourself on the map: identify the entrance, the fixed reference points, and the labels already provided. When the speaker says "turn left at the..." or "just past the..." — use the map's fixed points, not relative left/right (which depends on which direction the speaker is facing).

## Sections 3 and 4 — Advanced Candidate Issues

Section 4 (academic monologue) is where candidates at the 7.5–8 level lose marks they shouldn't. The content is more complex and the pace is faster, but the question types are usually completion — which are mechanical if you've predicted the answer type.

**The specific trap in Section 4:** The speaker frequently uses signpost language that sounds like a topic change but isn't ("Now, turning to..."). Candidates who treat every signpost as a cue to look up from the question sheet often miss the next answer.

**Strategy for Section 4:** Stay on the questions. Let the signposts pass through. Your anchor is the question sequence, not the lecture structure.

## Spelling and Answer Transfer

10 minutes are given at the end for answer transfer. This is not bonus time — it's a trap for candidates who are careless.

**Rules:**
- Both US and UK spelling are acceptable (colour/color, organisation/organization)
- Words in brackets are optional — write them if you're sure, omit them if uncertain
- Plurals must be correct
- Numbers can be written as digits or words unless the instruction specifies

**Transfer errors that cost marks:**
- Copying a word differently from how you wrote it during listening
- Adding extra words when the instruction specified a word count limit
- Illegible handwriting (examiners cannot give benefit of the doubt)

## Diagnostic: Identifying Where You're Losing Marks

After running a timed section, categorise every error:

| Error type | Likely cause | Fix |
|-----------|-------------|-----|
| Heard the answer but missed it | Not reading ahead | Stricter pre-listening prep |
| Chose the distractor in MCQ | Reactive matching (word-level) | Underline verb+subject in question |
| Correct semantic field, wrong word | Spelling or form error | Slow down during transfer |
| Simply didn't understand | Speed / accent / vocabulary | Section 4 vocabulary drilling |
| Wrong answer on map task | Spatial disorientation | Pre-orient before recording starts |

Band 8 candidates almost never lose marks due to comprehension in Sections 1–3. Errors are almost exclusively mechanical (distractor traps, spelling, pre-reading failure). Diagnose before drilling.
