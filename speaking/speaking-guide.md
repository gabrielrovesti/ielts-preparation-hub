# Speaking — Preparation Guide & Cue Card Bank

> **Status: TBD** — establish baseline with a full recorded mock before using this material.  
> Speaking is assessed on 4 criteria (same weighting as Writing): Fluency & Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammatical Range & Accuracy, Pronunciation.

---

## Test Structure

| Part | Format | Duration | What's assessed |
|------|--------|----------|----------------|
| Part 1 | Examiner Q&A on familiar topics (home, work, interests) | 4–5 min | Fluent production on personal/concrete topics |
| Part 2 | Cue card monologue + 1–2 follow-up questions | 3–4 min (1 min prep + 1–2 min talk) | Extended discourse, organisation, lexical range |
| Part 3 | Abstract discussion linked to Part 2 theme | 4–5 min | Analytical reasoning, arguing a position, handling challenge |

**Total: 11–14 minutes**

---

## Band Descriptors (Speaking) — What Separates 7 from 8

| Criterion | Band 7 | Band 8 |
|-----------|--------|--------|
| Fluency & Coherence | Speaks at length without noticeable effort; some repetition or self-correction | Speaks fluently with only occasional repetition; develops topics coherently |
| Lexical Resource | Uses vocabulary flexibly; occasional inaccuracies in less common items | Uses wide vocabulary resource fluently and flexibly; rare inaccuracies |
| Grammatical Range | Uses complex structures frequently; errors rarely cause difficulty | Wide range of structures; most sentences are error-free |
| Pronunciation | Easy to understand throughout; L1 accent does not impede | Wide range of features; easy to understand throughout |

**The 7→8 jump in practice:**
- Band 7: produces complex language with some errors
- Band 8: produces complex language **accurately and fluently**, with errors rare enough that they don't register as patterns

## Part 1 Strategy

Part 1 is not just a warm-up — it's 4–5 minutes of scored production. Common mistakes:

**Too short:** Giving one-sentence answers. The examiner will prompt more, but repeated prompting signals low fluency.

**Too mechanical:** Restating the question before answering ("Do I prefer X or Y? Well, I prefer X because..."). This is audible filler.

**Band 8 approach:** Answer directly, extend with one specific reason or anecdote, then land cleanly. 3–4 sentences per answer is the target.

**Structure:** `[Direct answer] + [Reason/example] + [Optional extension or contrast]`

Example (topic: food):
> *I'd say I'm a fairly adventurous eater. I grew up in a household where my mother cooked dishes from different regions, so I developed a taste for variety quite early. That said, there are certain comfort foods I always come back to — a good plate of pasta being the obvious one.*

## Part 2 — Cue Card Bank

**Protocol:**
1. Read the card and **all four bullet points** during the 1-minute prep
2. Use bullet points as paragraph anchors — don't treat them as a list to tick off
3. Aim for 1.5–2 minutes of continuous talk
4. Build a narrative arc: set the scene → develop → reflect/evaluate

**Band 8 move:** Don't just describe — **evaluate and reflect**. The final prompt ("and explain why...") is where band 7 candidates run out of things to say. Prepare a 30-second reflective close for every card type.

### SP2-11-1 | Home / Living Space

> Describe a house or apartment that someone you know lives in.

**You should say:**
- whose house/apartment this is
- where the house/apartment is
- what it looks like inside
- and explain what you like or dislike about this person's house/apartment

**Part 3 discussion themes:**
- Different types of home: popularity, house vs. apartment trade-offs, do people always want larger homes?
- Finding a place to live: difficulty in your country, renting vs. buying, age at which adults should leave family home

**Prep notes:**
- Concrete physical description increases authenticity — pick one real space
- The "like or dislike" prompt is the reflective close: connect it to a value (privacy, community, efficiency)

---

### SP2-11-2 | Person / Writer

> Describe a writer you would like to meet.

**You should say:**
- who the writer is
- what you know about this writer already
- what you would like to find out about him/her
- and explain why you would like to meet this writer

**Part 3 discussion themes:**
- Reading and children: popular book types, why some children don't read, how to encourage reading
- Reading for different purposes: speed reading, jobs requiring heavy reading, novels vs. factual books

**Prep notes:**
- "What you would like to find out" is the hardest prompt — don't say "their inspiration" generically. Identify a specific creative or intellectual question.
- Avoid the trap of just listing the writer's achievements — the examiner wants *your engagement* with them.

---

### SP2-11-3 | Weather / Experience

> Describe a day when you thought the weather was perfect.

**You should say:**
- where you were on this day
- what the weather was like on this day
- what you did during the day
- and explain why you thought the weather was perfect on this day

**Part 3 discussion themes:**
- Types of weather: which weather people dislike and why, weather-dependent jobs, seasonal festivals
- Weather forecasts: importance of forecasts, best information sources, difficulty of prediction

**Prep notes:**
- This is a sensory description task — use specific adjectives (temperature, light, humidity, texture)
- The final "why perfect" prompt connects to mood and context: the same weather can be perfect or miserable depending on circumstance. That's an interesting observation that signals analytical depth.

---

### SP2-11-4 | Media / Documentary

> Describe a TV documentary you watched that was particularly interesting.

**You should say:**
- what the documentary was about
- why you decided to watch it
- what you learnt during the documentary
- and explain why the TV documentary was particularly interesting

**Part 3 discussion themes:**
- Different types of TV programmes: popularity, game shows, TV as news source
- TV advertising: most-advertised product types, audience attention, regulation

**Prep notes:**
- High risk of vagueness ("it was interesting because it taught me a lot"). Be specific about what you learnt and why that specific knowledge surprised or challenged you.
- If you don't watch documentaries, pick a podcast, YouTube series, or online lecture — the examiner won't know the difference, and you'll have more genuine content.

---

### SP2-13-1 | Person / Entrepreneur

> Describe someone you know who has started a business.

**You should say:**
- who this person is
- what work this person does
- why this person decided to start a business
- and explain whether you would like to do the same kind of work as this person

**Part 3 discussion themes:**
- Choosing work: jobs young people avoid, teacher vs. parent career advice, whether money drives job choice
- Work-life balance: pressure on working hours, societal impact of poor work-life balance, strategies for governments and employers

**Prep notes:**
- "Whether you would like to do the same" is the evaluative close — answer it honestly and with a reason, not just yes/no.
- Part 3 on this card touches work-life balance (a topic where your notes already have a strong Task 2 essay position — leverage that analytical framework verbally).

---

### SP2-13-3 | Discussion / Work Context

> Describe an interesting discussion you had as part of your work or studies.

**You should say:**
- what the subject of the discussion was
- who you discussed the subject with
- what opinions were expressed
- and explain why you found the discussion interesting

**Part 3 discussion themes:**
- Discussing problems with others: value of discussion, friends vs. family for problem-sharing, limits of sharing widely
- Communication skills at work: important skills in meetings, effects of poor written communication, technology's future impact on workplace communication

**Prep notes:**
- This card plays to your professional background naturally — a real discussion from academic or consulting work will produce richer content than an invented one.
- Part 3 on technology's impact on communication is high-probability in Speaking exams and directly overlaps with Task 2 topics. Prepare 3–4 analytical points on this.

## Part 3 Strategy

Part 3 is where band 7 and band 8 diverge most sharply. It requires:

1. **Analytical depth** — not just personal opinion, but reasoning about social/systemic patterns
2. **Hypothetical and conditional structures** — "If X were to happen, then Y would likely follow..."
3. **Hedging and qualification** — "It depends largely on...", "In most contexts...", "The extent to which..."
4. **Challenging and nuancing** — agreeing partially: "There's certainly something to that, though I'd qualify it by saying..."

**The two failures at band 7:**
- Giving short, definitive answers without development
- Staying at the personal/anecdotal level when the question asks for societal analysis

### Response Framework for Part 3

```
[Direct position] → [Reason A] → [Reason B or qualifier] → [Concrete example or evidence] → [Conclusion or implication]
```

**Example** (Topic: Work-life balance):
> *I'd say the pressure has increased substantially, yes — at least in knowledge-economy jobs. The always-on expectation created by smartphones and remote work tools has effectively dissolved the boundary between work time and personal time. What's changed isn't just the hours people work, but the psychological inability to disengage. The long-term societal cost of that is measurable in burnout rates and declining productivity — which is somewhat ironic, given that overwork is usually justified in the name of productivity.*

## Self-Recording Protocol

Since Speaking feedback requires external observation, the only effective practice loop without a teacher is audio/video recording.

**Session structure:**
1. Select one cue card
2. Prep 1 minute (timer)
3. Record the full 2-minute response
4. Listen back once — note only the **top 3 issues** (don't try to fix everything)
5. Re-record once, targeting those specific issues

**What to listen for:**
- Filler sounds: "erm", "uhh", "like", "you know" — these cap Fluency & Coherence
- Vocabulary repetition — same word used twice in a paragraph is audible
- Grammatical errors on complex structures — you'll hear agreement or tense errors you don't notice when speaking
- Whether the "explain why" prompt is actually answered with reflection, not just description
