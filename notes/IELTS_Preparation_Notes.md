## 1. Band Scale Reference

| Band | Label | Description |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 9 | Expert | Full operational command. Fluent, accurate, and appropriate. Complete understanding. |
| 8 | Very Good | Fully operational command with only occasional unsystematic inaccuracies. |
| 7 | Good | Operational command with occasional inaccuracies and misunderstandings in some situations. |
| 6 | Competent | Generally effective command despite some inaccuracies and misunderstandings. |
| 5 | Modest | Partial command; copes with overall meaning in most situations. |
| 4 | Limited | Basic competence limited to familiar situations. |
| 3 | Extremely Limited | Only general meaning in familiar situations. |
| 2 | Intermittent | No real communication; isolated words in familiar situations. |
| 1 | Non-User | Essentially no ability. |

| Band | Typical Requirement |

## 2. Scoring Mechanics

Overall band = mean of the four component scores, **rounded to nearest 0.5**.

| Component | Weight |
|-----------|--------|
| Listening | 25% |
| Reading | 25% |
| Writing | 25% |
| Speaking | 25% |

**Key implication:** A single weak component pulls the overall band down significantly.

**Example:** Reading 8 + Listening 8 + Writing 7 + Speaking 7 → Mean 7.5 → **Overall 7.5**

**Rounding rule:** Means of x.25 and x.75 round **up**; x.0 and x.5 are exact.

**Score calculation example:**
- Listening: 8.0
- Reading: 8.5
- Writing: 7.5
- Speaking: 7.5
- Mean: 7.875 → rounds to **8.0**

> Writing is statistically the component most candidates underperform relative to actual proficiency. For candidates already at band 7+, explicit writing practice is the highest-yield investment.

## 3. Writing Task 1 — Letter (General Training)

### Task Parameters

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Time allocation | ~20 minutes |
| Minimum word count | 150 words |
| Format | No addresses. Begin with "Dear [Name]," |
| Register | Must match the relationship (formal / semi-formal / informal) |

### Mandatory Structure

All three bullet points in the task are **mandatory**. Missing one bullet drops the Task Achievement score.

**Recommended paragraph mapping:**

```
Para 1:  Opening + Bullet 1
Para 2:  Bullet 2
Para 3:  Bullet 3 + Closing
```

### Sample Task

> You have heard that one of your friends will be doing voluntary work at a festival. Write a letter to your friend. In your letter:
> - ask about what type of festival your friend is volunteering at and when and where it will take place
> - ask what your friend will have to do as part of the voluntary work
> - ask if the organisers are still looking for more volunteers and how you can apply for the work

### Model Answer (Band 7.0–7.5)

Dear Pat,

I was absolutely thrilled to hear that you will be volunteering at a festival this summer! It sounds like a wonderful experience, and I would love to find out more about it. Could you tell me what kind of festival it is — is it a music event, a cultural celebration, or something else entirely? I am also curious about when and where it will be held, as I imagine the location and timing would make quite a difference.

I would also love to know more about what the role actually involves. What sorts of tasks will you be expected to carry out as a volunteer? Are you working front-of-house, helping backstage, or something more logistical?

By the way, do you know if they still need more volunteers? I'd love to get involved if there's still a chance. Do you have any details on the application process?

Looking forward to hearing from you!

Best,  
[Name]

### Band Differentiation Analysis

| Transition | What Separates the Bands |
|-----------|--------------------------|
| 7 → 8 | Eliminate irrelevant content; ensure all questions are on-task; add specific follow-up per bullet |
| 8 → 9 | Inject personal voice; use informal language naturally (contractions, rhetorical questions, light humour); make the reader feel the letter is from a real person |
| Ceiling breaker | Offer options within a question ("Is it music, food, or cultural performances?") — shows lexical range without inflating word count |

> **Disputed score note:** Inventing facts not given in the prompt (e.g. assuming a location, date, or theme without being asked) is a Task Response failure. In a real exam this costs more than one band point.

### Examiner Criteria Weights (Task 1)

| Criterion | Weight |
|-----------|--------|
| Task Achievement | 25% |
| Coherence & Cohesion | 25% |
| Lexical Resource | 25% |
| Grammatical Range & Accuracy | 25% |

## 4. Writing Task 2 — Essay

### Task Parameters

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Time allocation | ~40 minutes |
| Minimum word count | 250 words |
| Target length | 280–320 words (sufficient for full Task Response) |
| Prompt type (example) | Opinion essay — "To what extent do you agree or disagree?" |

### Positioning Strategy

For *"to what extent do you agree or disagree?"* prompts:

- **Avoid binary stances** — a partial/nuanced position scores higher under Task Response and Coherence because it demonstrates analytical complexity.
- **Optimal position:** Acknowledge merits of one side, identify practical constraints, argue for a hybrid/nuanced conclusion.

### Recommended Structure

```
Introduction  →  State position clearly (1 paragraph)
Body 1        →  Strongest argument for one side (1 paragraph)
Body 2        →  Counter-argument / practical constraints (1 paragraph)
Conclusion    →  Restate nuanced position (1 paragraph)
```

Every body paragraph must have: **claim → explanation → example/evidence**

### Sample Task

> In many countries people have to pay for medical care, but some think that it should be a free service provided by the government. To what extent do you agree or disagree?

### Model Answer (Band 7.5–8.0)

The question of whether healthcare should be funded by the state or paid for by individuals is one of the most debated issues in modern public policy. While I recognise that fully free medical services present significant financial challenges, I largely agree that access to healthcare is a fundamental right and that governments bear a primary responsibility to ensure it is available to all citizens regardless of income.

The strongest argument in favour of government-funded healthcare is one of equity. When medical treatment is tied to personal wealth, those in the lowest income brackets are effectively penalised for being ill. Chronic conditions, which disproportionately affect lower-income populations due to environmental and lifestyle factors, become financially ruinous without state support. Countries such as the United Kingdom, with its National Health Service, demonstrate that universal coverage is operationally viable at scale.

However, the financial reality of fully free healthcare is non-trivial. Unlimited demand for a zero-cost service creates pressure on public budgets that can lead to long waiting times, underinvestment in infrastructure, and shortages of specialist staff. A purely state-funded model without any co-payment mechanism risks being fiscally unsustainable, particularly in ageing populations where healthcare consumption per capita rises steeply.

In conclusion, the most defensible position is a mixed model: a publicly funded baseline ensuring universal access to essential care, supplemented by regulated private options for those who can afford them. This balances the equity imperative with fiscal sustainability, and is in fact the approach adopted by most high-performing healthcare systems globally.

### Lexical Errors to Avoid (Italian Interference Patterns)

| Incorrect | Correct | Note |
|-----------|---------|------|
| demanded to | demanded that | False cognate — Italian *esigere che* uses subjunctive, not infinitive |
| provide equally | provide [X] equally / provide [X] | Adverb placement |
| very expensive | costly / prohibitively expensive | Avoid "very + basic adjective" at band 8+ |

## 5. Reading

### Question Types: Sentence Completion

**Core skill:** Identifying paraphrase — the passage rephrases the stem with different vocabulary but equivalent meaning. Answers that match surface words without paraphrase detection indicate a band 6–7 level reading profile.

### Paraphrase Trap Examples

| Stem phrase | Passage phrasing | Paraphrase mechanism |
|-------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| "excellent quality" | "high-quality teaching" | Adjective synonymy |
| "progression and development" | "progress" | Nominalisation collapse |
| "it is not possible to do" | "no [X] available" | Negation + structural change |

Navigating all three correctly indicates a band 8–8.5 reading profile.

> **Band signal:** Candidates who scan for matching words rather than meaning ceiling at band 6.5–7. True band 8 reading = meaning-first matching with tolerance for lexical variation.

## 7. Key Error Patterns to Drill

### Lexical Resource (Writing ceiling)

Italian syntactic interference under time pressure — these are the primary blockers for the **7.5 → 8.0 jump:**

- `demanded to` → `demanded that`
- `provide equally` → `provide [X] equally` / restructure
- `very [basic adjective]` → use precise synonyms (`costly`, `substantial`, `negligible`)

### Task 1 Register Calibration

Current draft register is slightly more formal than optimal for informal letters. Actions:

- Add one speculative option per question: *"Is it a music event, a food fair, or something cultural?"*
- Add one piece of personal context per paragraph to simulate natural conversation
- Use contractions freely in informal letters (`I'd`, `I've`, `it's`)

### Task 2 Positioning

- Never take a fully binary position on a *"to what extent"* prompt
- Lead with a clear directional stance in the introduction; qualify in body paragraphs; synthesise in conclusion
- Ensure every body paragraph has: **claim → explanation → example/evidence**
