# Lexical Resource Vault

> Primary bottleneck identified: Lexical Resource is the ceiling on both Writing Task 2 (currently 7.5–8.0) and Speaking (TBD).  
> This document addresses three distinct issues: (1) Italian interference patterns, (2) precision vocabulary by topic area, (3) prompt-to-body paraphrase strategies.

---

## Section 1 — Italian Interference Patterns

These are the highest-priority items. They surface under time pressure and are invisible to you as errors because they feel grammatically natural from an Italian syntactic baseline.

### Structural Interference

| Pattern | Italian source | Incorrect English | Correct English |
|---------|--------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Verb complement | *esigere/richiedere che* + congiuntivo | demanded **to** solve / required **to** do | demanded **that** X **solve** / required **that** X **do** |
| Adverb placement | *fornire ugualmente* | provide **equally** resources | provide resources **equally** / provide resources **in equal measure** |
| Verb deflation | nominale preference | *make a proposal* | **propose** |
| | | *make a decision* | **decide** |
| | | *have a discussion* | **discuss** |
| | | *give a recommendation* | **recommend** |
| False cognate | *sensibile* | **sensible** (= reasonable ✓) vs. **sensitive** (= emotionally reactive ✓) | use the correct word per context |
| False cognate | *eventualmente* | **eventually** (≠ possibly) | **eventually** = in the end; **possibly/potentially** = forse |
| False cognate | *attualmente* | **actually** misused for "currently" | **currently / at present / nowadays** |
| Quantifier | *la maggioranza* | **the majority of people** (vague, overused) | **most people / a significant proportion / the bulk of** |

## Section 2 — Topic-Area Vocabulary

These are the five most frequent IELTS Writing Task 2 topic domains. For each, the vocabulary is organised by argument position (supporting / opposing / qualifying) to match the typical essay structure.

---

### Topic 1: Healthcare & Public Health

**Core thesis vocabulary:**
- *universal access to healthcare* / *state-funded medical provision*
- *financial barrier to treatment* / *income-dependent healthcare*
- *preventive care* vs *curative treatment*
- *public health expenditure* / *per capita healthcare costs*
- *ageing population* / *rising demand for services*
- *co-payment mechanism* / *means-tested provision*
- *healthcare inequality* / *two-tier system*

**Supporting government-funded healthcare:**
> Healthcare access is a matter of **fundamental equity** rather than individual means. The correlation between low income and poor health outcomes is well-documented, creating a self-reinforcing cycle of poverty and illness that only **structural intervention** can break.

**Qualifying with fiscal constraints:**
> A **purely state-funded model**, however, faces significant pressure in contexts of rising demand and constrained public budgets. Without some form of **co-payment or regulated private supplementary provision**, the system risks being **fiscally unsustainable**, particularly where demographic ageing drives **disproportionate consumption** of high-cost interventions.

---

### Topic 2: Education

**Core thesis vocabulary:**
- *rote learning* vs *critical thinking* / *inquiry-based learning*
- *curriculum design* / *assessment framework*
- *vocational training* / *academic education*
- *lifelong learning* / *transferable skills*
- *educational attainment* / *knowledge acquisition*
- *pedagogical approach* / *learning outcomes*
- *digital literacy* / *technological competence*

**Supporting practical skills:**
> An **overemphasis on factual recall** at the expense of **applied problem-solving** produces graduates who are **ill-equipped** for the demands of the modern labour market. The capacity to **synthesise information, evaluate evidence**, and **adapt to novel contexts** — competencies that **rote learning** does not develop — has become increasingly central to professional effectiveness.

**Qualifying: facts are foundational:**
> However, **declarative knowledge** is not separable from the reasoning processes that operate on it. The argument is not that factual content should be eliminated, but that **assessment reform** — away from **terminal examinations** toward **continuous competency evaluation** — would better reflect the balance of skills that educational systems claim to develop.

---

### Topic 3: Technology & Communication

**Core thesis vocabulary:**
- *digital connectivity* / *information overload*
- *social media proliferation* / *algorithmic content curation*
- *erosion of privacy* / *data commodification*
- *automation displacement* / *labour market disruption*
- *remote working* / *always-on work culture*
- *digital divide* / *unequal access to technology*
- *misinformation / disinformation* (distinguish: misinformation = false; disinformation = deliberately false)

**Supporting regulation:**
> The **opacity of algorithmic systems** that determine information exposure creates a significant risk of **epistemic fragmentation** — where individuals inhabit information environments that **systematically confirm existing beliefs** rather than expose them to diverse perspectives. This argues for **regulatory intervention** in the design of content recommendation systems.

**Nuancing the benefits:**
> Technology has, simultaneously, **democratised access to information** at an unprecedented scale. The same infrastructure that enables misinformation also enables **independent journalism**, **peer-reviewed research dissemination**, and **civic organising** in contexts where traditional media is constrained or censored.

---

### Topic 4: Environment & Sustainability

**Core thesis vocabulary:**
- *carbon emissions* / *greenhouse gas* / *net-zero targets*
- *renewable energy transition* / *fossil fuel dependency*
- *environmental degradation* / *ecological impact*
- *sustainable development* / *circular economy*
- *intergenerational equity* / *future generations*
- *corporate accountability* / *regulatory framework*
- *individual behavioural change* vs *systemic policy change*

**Arguing for systemic over individual action:**
> The framing of environmental responsibility as **individual consumer choice** systematically **underestimates structural barriers** to sustainable behaviour and **deflects attention** from the **institutional sources** of emissions. The evidence consistently shows that **policy instruments** — carbon pricing, subsidy reform, mandatory standards — produce **orders of magnitude greater impact** than voluntary behavioural campaigns.

---

### Topic 5: Work & Society

**Core thesis vocabulary:**
- *work-life balance* / *occupational stress* / *burnout*
- *remote work* / *flexible working arrangements*
- *productivity* / *economic output*
- *gender pay gap* / *workplace inequality*
- *gig economy* / *precarious employment*
- *automation* / *job displacement*
- *minimum wage* / *income inequality*
- *corporate social responsibility*

**On work-life balance:**
> The **dissolution of the boundary between professional and personal time**, accelerated by the normalisation of **digital availability outside contracted hours**, constitutes a qualitative shift in the nature of employment rather than merely a quantitative increase in hours worked. The **psychological burden** of perpetual availability has been linked to measurable increases in **chronic stress, burnout rates**, and ultimately **reduced long-term productivity** — the precise outcome that extended working hours ostensibly seek to prevent.

## Section 3 — Prompt Paraphrase Strategies

In Task 2, the introduction and conclusion must paraphrase the prompt — never repeat its exact wording. This is a direct LR marker.

**Systematic approach:** Identify the key noun, verb, and adjective in the prompt, then substitute across all three dimensions.

### Worked Examples

**Prompt:** *"In many countries, people have to pay for medical care, but some think that it should be a free service provided by the government."*

| Element | Prompt wording | Paraphrase options |
|---------|---------------|-------------------|
| Topic noun | medical care | healthcare / medical treatment / health services |
| Verb | pay for | fund individually / bear the cost of / meet the expense of |
| Counter-concept | free service | state-funded provision / publicly financed system / universal coverage |
| Agent | government | the state / public authorities / the public sector |

**Paraphrased introduction opening:**
> *"The question of whether healthcare should be financed by the state or borne by individual patients is one of the most debated issues in contemporary public policy."*

---

**Prompt:** *"Some people say that in all levels of education, too much time is spent on learning facts and not enough on learning practical skills."*

| Element | Prompt wording | Paraphrase options |
|---------|---------------|-------------------|
| Topic | learning facts | memorising information / acquiring declarative knowledge / rote learning |
| Counter | practical skills | applied competencies / real-world capabilities / transferable skills |
| Qualifier | too much / not enough | a disproportionate emphasis / insufficient attention |
| Scope | all levels of education | across the entire educational system / from primary through tertiary |

**Paraphrased opening:**
> *"Educational systems have long been criticised for prioritising the acquisition of factual knowledge over the development of practical, applicable competencies."*

---

### Anti-Patterns to Eliminate

These introduction constructions actively lower the Task Response and LR scores:

| Avoid | Reason |
|-------|--------|
| "Nowadays, in today's modern society..." | Redundant, clichéd, visible to every examiner |
| "This is a very controversial topic..." | Vague filler; says nothing |
| "In my opinion, I think that I believe..." | Triple hedge — use one |
| "I will discuss both sides of this argument..." | Meta-commentary, not positioning |
| Repeating the prompt verbatim in the introduction | Zero LR credit |

## Section 4 — Active Drill: Upgrade Sentences

For each weak sentence, rewrite it at band 8 level. Focus on replacing underlined items.

**Drill (rewrite before checking the key):**

1. *"Air pollution is **very bad** for people's health and **many** countries have **big** problems with it."*

2. *"The government **demanded** companies **to reduce** their carbon emissions **very quickly**."*

3. *"It is **very important** that schools **provide equally** resources to all students."*

4. *"**Nowadays**, **a lot of** young people prefer to **use** technology instead of reading books."*

5. *"This has caused **very big** changes in how people **work** and **make** their lives."*

---

**Key:**

1. → *"Air pollution poses **significant** health risks, and **a considerable number of** countries face **substantial** challenges in managing it."*

2. → *"The government **required that** companies **reduce** their carbon emissions **rapidly** / **within a compressed timeline**."*

3. → *"It is **critical** that schools **distribute resources equitably** across all students."*

4. → *"**In recent decades**, an **increasing proportion of** young people have **opted for** digital media over print."*

5. → *"This has **precipitated considerable** changes in both **professional practice** and **lifestyle patterns**."*
