### T2-11-1 | Discussion | Personal Information in Hiring

Employers sometimes ask job applicants about personal details such as hobbies, interests, and relationship status. Some argue this information is legitimately relevant to hiring decisions; others believe it is an inappropriate intrusion.

**Discuss both views and give your own opinion.**

**Prompt type analysis:** Discussion + personal opinion. The challenge is giving a fair account of the "for" view even if you disagree — examiners penalise strawmanning.

**Suggested angle:** The "for" view has a genuine case (cultural fit, team dynamics, life balance indicators). The "against" view has a stronger case (discrimination risk, irrelevance, privacy norms). A nuanced position: relevant in some contexts (senior roles, team-heavy environments), inappropriate as a general practice.

**Strong example to develop:** Jurisdictions where asking about marital status in interviews is illegal (UK, US) — demonstrates the legal and ethical consensus against it, while acknowledging that voluntary disclosure is different.

### T2-11-2 | Opinion | Practical vs. Factual Education

A widely held position argues that educational systems at all levels, from primary through tertiary, allocate excessive time to memorising and reproducing facts at the expense of developing practical, applicable skills.

**Do you agree or disagree?**

**Prompt type analysis:** Opinion — "agree or disagree." A binary framing, but a nuanced position (agree with qualification) scores higher.

**Suggested angle:** Largely agree (factual recall dominates assessment design across most systems), but with a qualification: factual knowledge is a prerequisite for higher-order reasoning — the issue is the balance, not the presence of facts. The solution is assessment reform, not content removal.

**Strong examples:** Rote-learning dominated curricula in East Asian systems vs. inquiry-based learning in Nordic systems — contrasting outcomes on creative and applied skills metrics.

### T2-11-3 | Opinion | Images in News Media

News reporting on television and in print is increasingly image-heavy. Some argue that visual content is more powerful and communicative than text.

**What is your opinion?**

**Prompt type analysis:** Opinion. Avoid the trap of treating this as a pure comparison — the question asks for your position, not a "both sides" discussion.

**Suggested angle:** Partially agree — images have unique communicative properties (immediacy, emotional impact, cross-language accessibility) but are also more easily manipulated and decontextualised than text. Neither is inherently superior; their effectiveness is context-dependent.

**Strong examples:** Iconic photojournalism (establishing the power of images) vs. misleadingly cropped or AI-generated news images (establishing the risk of over-reliance).

### T2-11-4 | Opinion | Clothing as Cultural Indicator

A common view holds that an individual's clothing choices reliably communicate their cultural identity and character.

**Do you agree or disagree?**

**Prompt type analysis:** Opinion. Deceptively simple topic — the challenge is to build an analytical argument rather than anecdotal reasoning.

**Suggested angle:** Partially agree — clothing can signal cultural affiliation, group identity, and deliberate self-presentation, but it is an unreliable indicator of character. The relationship is mediated by context, socioeconomic constraints, and conscious versus default choice.

**Strong examples:** Uniform dress codes that suppress individual expression vs. subcultural clothing as identity assertion (e.g., professional dress in high-stakes settings); constraints on clothing choice due to climate, religion, or economic access.

### T2-13-1 | Opinion | Teenage Curfews

In some regions, adolescents below a certain age are prohibited from being outdoors at night without adult accompaniment.

**What is your opinion about this policy?**

**Prompt type analysis:** Opinion. The topic is politically charged — maintain analytical distance, avoid polemic.

**Suggested angle:** Partially support — there is a genuine public safety rationale in high-crime areas, and curfew policies have some evidential backing in reducing opportunistic crime. However, blanket curfews risk criminalising normal adolescent behaviour and may be implemented inequitably across demographic groups. A targeted, evidence-based approach is preferable to a blunt general restriction.

### T2-13-2 | Problem-Solution | Sedentary Working Adults

A significant proportion of employed adults report getting very little or no physical exercise, either within the working day or outside it, leading to documented health consequences.

**Why does this problem occur? What could be done to address it?**

**Prompt type analysis:** Two-part — causes then solutions. Critical rule: solutions must be tied to the specific causes identified, or the argument becomes incoherent.

**Suggested causes:** Time pressure from long working hours; sedentary job design; poor urban infrastructure; psychological barriers (low self-efficacy, fatigue).

**Matched solutions:** Regulatory limits on working hours; employer wellness programmes; urban planning (cycle infrastructure, walkable design); workplace exercise integration (standing desks, walking meetings).

### T2-13-3 | Discussion | Streaming vs. Cinema

Streaming services have made films accessible on personal devices anywhere. Some argue this eliminates the need for the cinema as an institution; others maintain that the cinema experience is irreplaceable and that films are designed to be seen there.

**Discuss both views and give your own opinion.**

**Prompt type analysis:** Discussion + opinion. Fair representation of both sides is required.

**"No need for cinema" view:** Convenience, cost, accessibility, personalisation of the viewing experience.

**"Cinema is irreplaceable" view:** Screen scale, audio quality, communal experience, distraction-free environment, social ritual.

**Nuanced personal position:** The two are not direct substitutes — they serve different psychological and social functions. Cinema will contract but not disappear; it will specialise in event films and communal experience, while streaming dominates everyday consumption.

### T2-13-4 | Opinion | Tidiness in Home and Workplace

The view is held by many that maintaining orderly, well-organised environments — both at home and at work — is important and beneficial.

**What is your opinion about this?**

**Prompt type analysis:** Opinion. Seemingly straightforward but easy to write superficially.

**Suggested angle:** Largely agree — physical organisation reduces cognitive load, supports task-switching efficiency, and correlates with reduced stress in workplace settings. However, the optimal level of tidiness is individual and context-dependent: creative environments often benefit from controlled disorder (studies on messy desks and divergent thinking). The claim that tidiness is universally important is an overstatement.

**Strong examples:** Systematic workplace organisation (5S methodology in manufacturing); research on environmental disorder and creative performance (Vohs et al., 2013).
