### T1-11-1 | Informal | Career Advice

A friend has written asking whether they should go to college or try to find a job. You believe they should pursue employment.

**Write a letter to this friend. In your letter:**
- explain why you think college is not the right fit for them at this point
- give reasons why getting a job would be beneficial for them specifically
- suggest some types of work that might suit their situation

*Begin: Dear [Name],*

**Difficulty notes:** Informal register throughout. Avoid being preachy — frame as advice, not instruction. The three points are distinct enough that missing one is easy under time pressure.

**Register trap:** This is informal (a friend, not a colleague). Use contractions, personal tone, and hedging language ("I reckon...", "it seems to me..."). A formal structure here would cap Task Achievement.

---

### T1-11-2 | Formal | Lost Property at a Hotel

After attending a meeting at a hotel, you discovered on returning home that you had left some important papers there.

**Write a letter to the hotel manager. In your letter:**
- indicate where you believe you may have left the papers
- explain the nature and importance of these documents
- state what action you would like the manager to take

*Begin: Dear Sir or Madam,*

**Difficulty notes:** Formal register. The prompt asks for three quite different things — location inference (uncertain), importance explanation (factual), and a clear request (directive). Students often conflate bullets 2 and 3.

**Band 8 move:** Be specific and confident about the location (e.g., "the main conference room on the second floor"), even though the prompt says "you think" — hedging with "I believe" is fine, but vagueness about location is a TA weakness.

---

### T1-11-3 | Formal | Sports Centre Closure

The local council is considering closing a publicly funded sports and leisure centre to reduce costs.

**Write a letter to the local council. In your letter:**
- describe how you and people you know currently use the centre
- explain why the centre matters to the local community beyond individual use
- outline the likely consequences for residents if the closure goes ahead

*Begin: Dear Sir or Madam,*

**Difficulty notes:** Formal. Three bullets with a logical escalation: personal use → community value → consequences. Natural for a persuasive argument structure.

**Band 8 move:** The strongest candidates make the community argument systemic (public health costs, social isolation for elderly residents) rather than anecdotal. The consequences paragraph should be forward-projecting and concrete.

### T1-11-4 | Formal | Training Course Request

While working for an international company, you have found a training course that would be relevant to your professional development.

**Write a letter to your manager. In your letter:**
- describe the training course and what it covers
- explain what support you are requesting from the company
- outline how completing the course would benefit your work

*Begin: Dear Sir or Madam,*

**Difficulty notes:** Semi-formal/formal. This is a workplace request — professional but not cold. The third bullet (benefit to the company) is where candidates underperform: they write about personal benefit rather than framing it as ROI for the organisation.

### T1-13-1 | Semi-formal | Secondment Request

You work for an international company and would like to spend six months at its head office in another country.

**Write a letter to your manager. In your letter:**
- explain your motivation for wanting to work at the head office for this period
- describe how your current responsibilities could be covered in your absence
- request your manager's assistance in making the arrangement possible

*Begin: Dear [Manager's name],*

**Difficulty notes:** Semi-formal (you know the manager). Tone should be professional but warmer than a formal complaint. The most challenging bullet is the second — candidates often write vaguely about "colleagues taking over" without specifying a viable handover structure.

---

### T1-13-2 | Formal | Hotel Meeting — Food Complaint

You organised a full-day company meeting at a hotel. Participants gave positive feedback about the venue overall, but were dissatisfied with the food served at lunch.

**Write a letter to the hotel manager. In your letter:**
- describe what participants appreciated about the hotel
- explain the specific nature of their dissatisfaction with the food
- suggest concrete changes the hotel could make to improve the food offering in future

*Begin: Dear Sir or Madam,*

**Difficulty notes:** Formal complaint, but not aggressive — the first bullet requires you to start on a positive note. Candidates who jump straight to the complaint fail the register and coherence criteria. The three bullets form a deliberate structure: praise → problem → solution.

---

### T1-13-3 | Formal | Rubbish Collection Complaint

The rubbish/garbage collection system in your local area is malfunctioning and causing problems for residents.

**Write a letter to the local council. In your letter:**
- describe how the collection system has been failing
- explain the impact this has had on you and your neighbours
- propose what action should be taken to resolve the situation

*Begin: Dear Sir or Madam,*

**Difficulty notes:** Similar structure to T1-11-3 but complaint-focused rather than persuasion-focused. Keep factual and specific. The impact paragraph is where register errors appear — emotional language ("this is disgusting") reads as informal; "this poses a significant risk to public health" is the appropriate register.

### T1-13-4 | Informal | Job Recommendation

A friend you made while studying abroad has written asking for help finding work in your country. You have come across a local vacancy that could suit them.

**Write a letter to this friend. In your letter:**
- describe the job and the type of work involved
- explain why you think this opportunity is appropriate for them
- give them the information they need to submit an application

*Begin: Dear [Name],*

**Difficulty notes:** Informal. The second bullet is the hardest — you need to make the recommendation feel personal and reasoned, not generic. Reference something specific about your friend's background or skills.

**Register trap:** Students often slip into formal job-description language when describing the role. Keep the vocabulary conversational — "basically you'd be..." rather than "the responsibilities include..."
